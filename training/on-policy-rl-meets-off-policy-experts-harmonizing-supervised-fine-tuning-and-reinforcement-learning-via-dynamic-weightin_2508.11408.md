## Abstract
**[Key point]**:
- The paper addresses the tension between Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) and Reinforcement Learning (RL) in LLM post-training.
- Existing integration methods risk disrupting established response patterns and causing overfitting to static expert data.
- The authors propose a unified view of SFT and RL through the lens of off-policy versus on-policy learning.

**Main contribution**:
- Introduction of CHORD (Controllable Harmonization of On- and Off-Policy Reinforcement Learning via Dynamic Weighting).
- Reframing SFT not as a distinct pre-RL stage but as a dynamically weighted auxiliary objective within the on-policy RL loop.
- Development of a dual-control mechanism involving global coefficients and token-wise weighting functions.

**Key results:**
- CHORD achieves stable and efficient learning by harmonizing off-policy expert data with on-policy exploration.
- Significant performance improvements over baseline methods across various practical tasks.
- Empirical evidence shows mitigation of overfitting and disruption of response patterns.

**Conclusion:**
- Effective harmonization of off-policy and on-policy signals is crucial for robust LLM alignment.
- The proposed dynamic weighting strategy provides a scalable and effective solution for post-training.
- Code implementation is released to encourage further research in this area.

## 1 Introduction
**Context:**
- Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) and Reinforcement Learning (RL) are the two dominant paradigms for post-training LLMs.
- SFT aligns models with human preferences using static datasets, while RL optimizes for long-term rewards using dynamic interactions.
- Combining these paradigms is challenging due to conflicting optimization objectives and data distributions.
- Standard approaches often treat SFT and RL as sequential stages, which can lead to catastrophic forgetting or instability.

**Goal:**
- To investigate a unified framework that seamlessly integrates SFT and RL.
- To address the specific risks of using off-policy expert data during on-policy RL training.
- To propose a method that dynamically balances imitation (SFT) and exploration (RL).

**Contributions:**
- Theoretical analysis of the interaction between off-policy data and on-policy policies.
- Proposal of the CHORD framework with a novel dynamic weighting mechanism.
- Comprehensive empirical evaluation demonstrating superior stability and performance.
- Release of open-source implementation for reproducibility.

## 2 Preliminaries
**Context:**
- Defines the mathematical notation for LLMs, policies, and reward models.
- Reviews standard SFT and RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback) formulations.
- Establishes the distinction between on-policy and off-policy learning in the context of LLMs.

**Key Concepts:**
- **On-Policy Learning**: Data generated by the current policy being trained (e.g., PPO).
- **Off-Policy Learning**: Data generated by a different policy (e.g., expert demonstrations or historical data).
- **KL Divergence**: Used to measure the distance between the current policy and reference policy.
- **Reward Shaping**: Techniques to modify rewards to guide learning more effectively.

**Problem Formulation:**
- The goal is to maximize expected reward while maintaining proximity to the reference model.
- Standard RLHF optimizes $E[\sum r_t]$ subject to KL constraints.
- SFT minimizes cross-entropy loss on expert trajectories.
- The challenge lies in jointly optimizing these objectives without destabilizing the learning process.

## 3 Chord: Harmonizing Off-Policy and On-Policy Learning
**Context:**
- Introduces the core methodology of CHORD.
- Explains how SFT is integrated as an auxiliary objective within the RL loop.
- Details the dual-control mechanism: global coefficient $\mu$ and token-wise function $\phi(\cdot)$.

### 3.1 The Shift-Readapt-Overfit Progression When Utilizing Off-Policy Data
**Context:**
- Analyzes the negative effects of naively mixing off-policy expert data with on-policy RL.
- Identifies three stages of degradation: Shift, Readapt, and Overfit.

**Shift Phase:**
- Initial introduction of off-policy data causes a sudden shift in policy distribution.
- The model attempts to mimic expert behavior, deviating from its current on-policy trajectory.
- This leads to instability in the early stages of training.

**Readapt Phase:**
- The model struggles to reconcile the conflicting signals from SFT and RL.
- On-policy exploration is suppressed as the model prioritizes imitation.
- Reward performance may temporarily degrade as the model "unlearns" RL-optimized behaviors.

**Overfit Phase:**
- Prolonged exposure to static expert data causes the model to overfit to the specific distribution of the expert.
- The model loses generalization capabilities and becomes rigid.
- Performance on out-of-distribution or novel tasks declines significantly.

**Implication:**
- Direct concatenation of SFT and RL data is suboptimal.
- Dynamic control of the influence of off-policy data is necessary.

### 3.2 Controlling the Influence of Off-Policy Expert Data via μ
**Context:**
- Introduces the global coefficient $\mu$ to regulate the overall weight of the SFT objective.
- $\mu$ acts as a scalar multiplier for the SFT loss term in the combined objective function.

**Mechanism:**
- The combined loss is defined as $L_{total} = L_{RL} + \mu \cdot L_{SFT}$.
- $\mu$ is not static but dynamically adjusted during training.
- High $\mu$ encourages strong imitation of expert data.
- Low $\mu$ prioritizes on-policy exploration and reward maximization.

**Dynamic Adjustment Strategy:**
- $\mu$ is decayed over time to reduce the influence of off-policy data as the policy stabilizes.
- Early training stages use higher $\mu$ to provide strong guidance.
- Later stages use lower $\mu$ to allow the model to explore and refine its own strategies.
- This prevents the "Overfit" phase identified in Section 3.1.

**Theoretical Justification:**
- Balances the bias-variance trade-off.
- Ensures that the policy does not deviate too far from the on-policy distribution.
- Facilitates a smooth transition from imitation to optimization.

### 3.3 Enhancing the Stability of Off-policy Learning via ϕ(·)
**Context:**
- Introduces the token-wise weighting function $\phi(\cdot)$ for granular control.
- Addresses the limitation of global weighting by allowing per-token adaptation.

**Mechanism:**
- $\phi(\cdot)$ assigns different weights to different tokens in the expert trajectory.
- Tokens that are more informative or critical for the task receive higher weights.
- Noisy or less relevant tokens receive lower weights, reducing their impact.

**Design of ϕ(·):**
- Based on the confidence of the current policy in generating the expert token.
- If the policy agrees with the expert, the weight is high.
- If the policy disagrees, the weight is low, preventing the model from being forced into incorrect behaviors.
- Can also be based on the difficulty of the token or its contribution to the final reward.

**Benefits:**
- Mitigates the risk of overfitting to noisy or incorrect expert demonstrations.
- Allows the model to learn from the "spirit" of the expert rather than rigidly copying every token.
- Enhances stability by smoothing the loss landscape.

**Integration with μ:**
- The final SFT loss is weighted by both $\mu$ and $\phi(\cdot)$.
- $L_{SFT}^{weighted} = \sum_{t} \mu_t \cdot \phi_t \cdot L_{token}(t)$.
- This dual-control mechanism provides fine-grained and coarse-grained control over the learning process.

## 4 Experiments
**Context:**
- Describes the experimental setup, datasets, and baselines.
- Presents results comparing CHORD with standard SFT, RLHF, and other hybrid methods.
- Analyzes the ablation studies and hyperparameter sensitivity.

### 4.1 Setup
**Datasets:**
- **Mathematical Reasoning**: GSM8K, MATH.
- **Code Generation**: HumanEval, MBPP.
- **Instruction Following**: AlpacaEval, WildChat.
- **Tool Use**: ToolBench.

**Baselines:**
- **SFT**: Standard supervised fine-tuning.
- **RLHF**: Standard PPO-based reinforcement learning.
- **DPO**: Direct Preference Optimization.
- **SFT+RL**: Sequential application of SFT followed by RL.
- **Mix-SFT**: Concatenation of SFT and RL data with static weights.

**Implementation Details:**
- Base models: Llama-2, Qwen, Mistral.
- Reward models: Rule-based for math/code, LLM-as-a-judge for instruction following.
- Training hardware: Multiple GPUs with distributed training.
- Hyperparameters: Learning rates, batch sizes, KL coefficients.

### 4.2 Comparisons
**Performance Metrics:**
- Accuracy on test sets.
- Pass@k for code generation.
- Win rate on AlpacaEval.
- Reward scores from the reward model.

**Key Findings:**
- CHORD outperforms all baselines in terms of stability and final performance.
- Sequential SFT+RL shows significant performance drops in later stages due to overfitting.
- Mix-SFT with static weights fails to adapt to the changing policy distribution.
- CHORD achieves higher reward scores with lower KL divergence, indicating better alignment.

**Task-Specific Results:**
- **Math**: CHORD shows superior reasoning capabilities and fewer hallucinations.
- **Code**: CHORD generates more syntactically correct and logically sound code.
- **Instruction Following**: CHORD produces more diverse and helpful responses.

### 4.3 Analysis on the Effects of μ and ϕ(·)
**Ablation Study:**
- Removing $\mu$ dynamic adjustment leads to instability.
- Removing $\phi(\cdot)$ leads to overfitting to noisy data.
- Both components are necessary for optimal performance.

**Sensitivity Analysis:**
- Varying the initial value of $\mu$.
- Varying the decay rate of $\mu$.
- Varying the form of $\phi(\cdot)$ (e.g., linear vs. exponential).

**Observations:**
- A slower decay of $\mu$ is beneficial for complex tasks.
- $\phi(\cdot)$ based on policy confidence is more effective than random weighting.
- The interaction between $\mu$ and $\phi(\cdot)$ is synergistic.

### 4.4 Further Analysis
**Computational Efficiency:**
- CHORD adds minimal overhead compared to standard RLHF.
- The dynamic weighting is computationally cheap to calculate.

**Generalization:**
- CHORD performs well on unseen tasks within the same domain.
- Robust to variations in the quality of expert data.

**Comparison with Other Hybrid Methods:**
- Superior to methods using curriculum learning.
- More stable than methods using importance sampling.

## 6 Conclusions and future directions
**Context:**
- Summarizes the main findings of the paper.
- Discusses limitations and future research directions.

**Summary:**
- CHORD effectively harmonizes SFT and RL through dynamic weighting.
- The dual-control mechanism of $\mu$ and $\phi(\cdot)$ is crucial for stability.
- Empirical results demonstrate significant improvements over baselines.

**Limitations:**
- Requires careful tuning of hyperparameters.
- Performance depends on the quality of the expert data.
- Computational cost is higher than pure SFT or pure RL.

**Future Directions:**
- Extending CHORD to multi-modal models.
- Investigating automated hyperparameter tuning for $\mu$ and $\phi(\cdot)$.
- Applying CHORD to other domains such as robotics and game playing.
- Exploring theoretical guarantees for the convergence of CHORD.
